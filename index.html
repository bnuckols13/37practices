37 Bodhisattva
Practices

In the opening months of Russia's full-scale invasion of Ukraine, a practitioner named Olga Kornyushyna finished writing a commentary on the 37 Bodhisattva Practices. She had started it before the war. She finished it inside it.

The book — read in bomb shelters and in exile, recommended alongside Viktor Frankl as a mental health resource during the war — does not yet exist in English. Your study group is the first step toward changing that.

"I highly recommend to study and contemplate The 37 Bodhisattva Practices to all my students. This text is my representative. If you act in accordance with the 37 Bodhisattva Practices teaching and never deviate from it, you will bring immeasurable benefit to yourself and to all sentient beings."

— Garchen Rinpoche

How it works

Three steps. No budget or credentials required.

01

Form a group

Gather 3 to 10 people from your sangha or practice community. Meet monthly or biweekly — in person or on Zoom. One practice per session. The root text is free to download.

02

Meet Olga

After a few sessions, invite Olga to join your group via Zoom. She'll share what it was like to write this commentary during wartime and sit with you as a fellow practitioner.

03

Fund the translation

When enough groups have formed and met Olga, we hold a Zoom event and make the ask. Your group's participation is what makes that ask real — not abstract.

About the book

37 Buddhist Practices: How to Survive Turbulent Times is a 310-page commentary on Thogme Zangpo's 14th-century text — one of the most important in the Tibetan Buddhist tradition, placed by Kyabjé Trulshik Rinpoche as the middle text of the lojong (mind training) canon.

The book weaves classical teachings with modern psychology and neuroscience, written in accessible, non-religious language. It is a practitioner's account of how these teachings function in a life during extreme difficulty — not a dry scholarly commentary.

It currently exists only in Ukrainian. This campaign is the first step toward an English edition.

About Olga

Olga Kornyushyna is a Ukrainian author and long-term Tibetan Buddhist practitioner. She began writing this commentary before Russia's full-scale invasion and completed it in the war's opening months.

She is open to joining study groups via Zoom — not as a formal teacher, but as a fellow practitioner who wrote these words under conditions most of us will never face.

To invite Olga to your group, register above and email Brian once your group has met two or three times.
